OC Habitats

Time: October 2021 - June 2022
Role: UX Designer
Tools Used: Figma, Slack

Project Context

About The Nonprofit

OC Habitats (OCH) is a nonprofit local to the Orange County area. Their mission is to protect, conserve, and rehabilitate nearby habitats and the species living within them. One way they do this is via “habitat monitoring”, where they assign volunteers to survey shorebirds on the Orange County coast.

About My Role

I worked as a UX designer with 6 other designers and 18 developers. This project was done as a part of Commit The Change at UCI, a student organization that partners with nonprofits to create high quality software. As a part of the OCH design team, I conducted user research, created designs from sketching to high fidelity prototypes, led user testing, and communicated with stakeholders.

About The Problem

OCH currently uses paper forms, or “monitor logs”, for their volunteers to record data about the local habitat. With 40+ logs per month, it is difficult to take note of data trends. The COVID-19 lockdown has also made it difficult to keep track of all the logs.

To solve this problem, we created a platform for OCH admins and volunteers to digitize their pen and paper monitor log system.

User Research

Before starting on designs, our team took the time to learn more about the users. The users for this system includes the admins, who will manage volunteers and form data, and volunteers, who will fill in monitor logs for the habitats they monitor. We gathered information about each group and what set of features each group needs.

What we know about the volunteers…

  • OCH averages 10-20 volunteers monthly
  • On average, each volunteer does 2 reports per month
  • Their volunteers are in the 30+ age group, many of whom are seniors
  • Some will fill out the form on their mobile or tablet devices
  • Some will use the paper form at the site, and transfer it to the platform later
  • As they survey the habitat, they will be using other devices as well (e.g. GPS device)

I was assigned to work on the admin-facing feature of editing the monitor log template. Every monitor log follows the same template, but on occasion, the form fields may be edited to reflect changes to the types of data being recorded. To perform additional research to design this feature, I conducted a competitive analysis on existing form builders on the market.

Design Process

Low Fidelity

Starting the designs on the edit monitor log feature proved to be difficult. In an attempt to give the OCH admins as much flexibility as possible, I created a design that would allow admins to create new sections and add questions of any type. However, there was a huge variety in question types, including dropdowns, dates, times, and special units like temperature, wind, tide, etc. This design started to become overwhelming, and felt like many traditional form editors.

Mid Fidelity

To learn more about the feature I was designing, I continued to ask the OCH admins about the scope and the context in which it would be used. This included questions such as how often they planned to edit the template, and what kind of changes they foresaw. With more clarity from our nonprofit stakeholders, I was able to gain a better understanding overall.

This allowed me to focus on the necessary features and make them as easy to use as possible. I decided on a fixed sidebar design so that each question could be selected and reviewed with ease. Drag and drop was also utilized to reorder questions and drag newly created questions to the desired spot. Other pages in the platform utilized drag and drop to move elements around, so this was maintained on the edit monitor log feature for consistency.

Style Guide

Stakeholder Feedback

With the completion of mid fidelity wireframes, my team and I conducted remote user testing with both internal (OCH admins and volunteers) and external (third party participants) user groups. I led 2 usability tests on Zoom using both desktop and mobile prototypes.

Feedback For “Edit Monitor Log” Page

  • Drag and drop felt unintuitive
  • Previewing a newly created question in the sidebar is not needed

Popups were chosen for the new design as…

  1. this feature was necessary but would be used infrequently
  2. a popup design would easily translate into a mobile design
  3. some of the sidebar features were already anticipated to be changed due to user feedback
  4. it would allow the developers to reuse components, and thus save time

What I've Learned...

We are not our users

Conducting usability tests with users of different backgrounds reinforced to me the importance of the user’s opinion. While my design team and I were excited to include features such as drag and drop, the users preferred an alternate method for this application. The feedback gathered from users during this process were crucial in the finalization of our designs.

Time constraints sometimes mean compromise

This project quickly became “overscoped”, with more features than we originally anticipated. To solve this, we had to simplify many of the features on the application, including the one I oversaw. As a result, the wireframes for this project looked drastically different at every stage. This experience taught me the importance of the needs of all stakeholders, not just the user, but also the project managers and developers.
